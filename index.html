Introduction

Artificial intelligence (AI), specifically generative artificial intelligence (GenAI), is a tool with potential to enhance  effectiveness and efficiency in teaching and learning. However, this field is fast paced and there may be potential for misuse and abuse without proper guidance.

These resources will serve as a preliminary guide that establishes working parameters for Artificial Intelligence within instruction at Mohave Community College. This includes defining AI, effectively and safely applying AI to teaching and learning, and addressing ethical considerations of AI in higher education.

It is not the scope of this guide to explore the technical aspects of AI. The guide will focus on what the taskforce views as best practices for engaging with GenAI in higher education. The guide will also seek to follow federal laws, best practices, and internal policies and procedures. Due to the constant evolution of these tools, this guide may be updated and revised as the technology and pedagogy advance.

Faculty are encouraged to approach AI with a growth mindset in teaching and learning. This means believing that one's abilities can improve through learning over time. One should engage with AI as an opportunity to learn rather than something to fear or avoid.

The growth mindset was popularized by Stanford psychology professor, Carol Dweck.

This guide was developed by a taskforce representing all instructional divisions, the Center for Teaching and Learning, Information Technology, and Employee Services.

1.1 Definitions

2 min read

Cornell University defines generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) as "a subset of AI that utilizes machine learning models to create new, original content, such as images, text, or music, based on patterns and structures learned from existing data."

Drawbacks and Considerations

Similar to other technologies, there are pros and cons of GenAI. Below are some drawbacks and considerations when using GenAI:

Bias

GenAI models mimic the data on which they are trained. Training data often contains mostly voices from majority populations. Without intentional diversification of training data, GenAI models will amplify majority voices, opinions, and sentiments.

Disinformation

Most text-based GenAI models, like ChatGPT, do not search for relevant sources in databases online. Instead, they use advanced statistics to predict the most likely next word – one word at a time. This can lead to inaccurate responses, often called hallucinations.

It also allows malicious actors to create realistic images, documents, and sources that may be used to intentionally deceive people.

Privacy

Review data use policies of the GenAI tool you plan to use. Only input data that are appropriate to share publicly and externally to MCC. Exercise caution when working with private, sensitive, or identifiable information, and avoid sharing any student information (which could be a FERPA violation), third party copyrighted materials, or any materials that you do not own or manage the rights to.

(Paraphrased from the UNLV Library)

Caution

Text Generative AI Tools

Text generative AI tools, or 'text-to-text', are tools that receive text inputs from the user and generate text outputs to the user. They can often sound human-like in their responses and will present themselves as helpful assistants. Some popular text-to-text tools are:
  •  ChatGPT is arguably the most well-known GenAI tool out there. It is a chatbot developed by OpenAI. There is a free version (ChatGPT 3.5) and a paid version (ChatGPT 4), which has more features and is tied to other GenAI tools, like DALL-E 3 (see image generative tools).
  •  Claude is GenAI chatbot developed by Anthropic. Anthropic prides themselves on their Constitutional AI model that is employed in Claude. According to Anthropic, Constitutional AI "AI enables AI systems to generate useful responses while also minimizing harm." Claude also has a free version and a paid version with more features.
  •  Google Gemini (formerly Google Bard) is Google's GenAI chatbot. You may interact with the chatbot on the web. However, Google has been integrating Gemini into more of it's products, like Google Assistant and Maps, to improve the user experience. Gemini is free.
  •  Microsoft Copilot is Microsoft's GenAI chatbot. Copilot is powered by OpenAI's ChatGPT-4 and DALL-E 3, which you normally have to pay for, but is free when running through Copilot. Microsoft has integrated Copilot into Windows desktops, the Edge browser, Bing, and their own mobile app. However, there are paid features of Copilot such as integration with Microsoft 365 apps and use of a faster model.

Image Generative Tools

Image generative tools, or 'text-to-image', receive text inputs from the user and generate images to the user that match the description. The text inputs can be natural language descriptions of images such as "An astronaut riding cowboy riding a horse" (seriously, try that out). Some popular text-to-image tools are:
  •  Stable Diffusion is a text-to-image model developed by Stability AI. Unlike most AI models, they released their source code to the public, providing more transparency and customization to users. Stable Diffusion also has an image editing tool. Stable Diffusion is free for users, and they offer some paid services to developers leveraging their model. Dream Studio is a sleek interface that leverages the Stable Diffusion model.
  •  Midjourney is a paid text-to-image model developed by an independent research lab solely focused on this one product. Midjourney is generally seen as one of the leaders in the field of text-to-image AIs.
  •  ImageFX is Google's text-to-image model. The tools is free and easy-to-use tool that is more accessible than most text-to-image models. However, it does not provide as much customization and freedom as other tools.
  •  DALL-E is a text-to-image model developed by OpenAI (the same developers of ChatGPT). It runs on the same model as ChatGPT. OpenAI is focusing their efforts to their newer model, DALL-E 3, which is part of the paid subscription package of ChatGPT. However, users can generate images on the DALL-E 3 model by using Microsoft Copilot for free.

Media Editing Tools

Video and voice editing and generating models are not as available as text and image generative models due to their complexity. There are some text-to-video models in development, but not readily available to the public. Below is a list of some media (video/voice) AI tools:
  •  Adobe Premiere Pro is a widely used video editing platform that has been developing their own AI features for video editing. It includes features such as speech-to-text, speech enhancement, scene detection, and more. Adobe Premiere Pro is a paid product; however, MCC has limited licenses available for employees. Submit an IT ticket to request an account.
  •  Murf AI is a text-to-speech tool specifically designed for media creation and voice overs on videos. There is a free version with very basic features, and paid licenses with more features and integrations.
  •  Synthesia is an AI video generator that uses AI avatars in place of actors or voice overs. The avatars themselves are each based on a real actor who has licensed their likeness to Synthesia. The tool is only available with a paid subscription.

2.2 Using AI to Develop Student Learning Outcomes

2 min read

Instructors can leverage Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) capabilities for the development of learning objectives and outcomes that are aligned with specific program goals. Utilizing GenAI can streamline the development process, ensuring that objectives are clear, measurable, and aligned with desired student learning outcomes. Having clear, measurable and aligned objectives and outcomes ensure better evaluation of program, course and student success.

Potential Uses

When using GenAI it is important to identify specific parameters in developing or enhancing learning objectives and outcomes. Some things that GenAI may be helpful with are:  

  • Language revisions for course package updates  
    • Ensuring proper writing conventions, like grammar and sentence structure, are used 
    • Ensuring that the objectives and outcomes are written with conciseness 
    • Ensuring that the Bloom's Taxonomy verbs are measurable & observable for each objective and outcome  
  • Convert an itemized list of topics to outcome statement sentences 
  • Brainstorm new outcomes for a new course  
  • Brainstorm objectives aligned to outcomes

Drawbacks

Although GenAI offers a variety of capabilities, there are some drawbacks to relying on GenAI in the development of objectives and outcomes.  

  • Lacks a holistic picture of program or prior knowledge of students 
  • Out of tune with the needs of our students, transfer partners, or degree requirements 
  • Creates responses that are wordy and technical 
  • Difficulty with creating measurable "action" words/verbs 
  • Without exposure to relevant content, it may provide incorrect information 

Because of these drawbacks, it is advised to revise and edit to meet the criteria of the course and student mastery levels.  

Guidelines and Examples

Provide Context

  • Course title 
  • Program student learning outcomes 
  • Students' prior knowledge 
  • Current outcomes, if applicable 
  • Role play 
    • "You are an expert in curriculum and assessment" 
    • "You are a college student. Are these outcomes clear?" 

Define Constraints

  • Number of outcomes or objectives 
  • Output format 
  • "Use Bloom's taxonomy" 

Use Outputs as Starting Point

  • Continue prompting for refined outputs 
  • Revise for better language 
  • Exclude irrelevant information

2.3 Assessment Creation in a World with AI

3 min read

Because Generative AI (GenAI) has the ability to rapidly create content, AI literacy is becoming more necessary in various fields, including education. This technology continues to impact and redefine industries students are preparing to enter after college and if they are not taught how to use it efficiently and ethically, they are in danger of being left behind. GenAI can be a powerful tool for the augmentation of an instructor's current assessments or provide ideas for new assessments that provide exposure to GenAI for students and teach them to use it to enhance their learning. 

Assistance with Assessment Creation

Creating assessments with GenAI has several benefits including the ability to be quickly recreated every semester and avoid answers being readily available online. Also, instructors can use the tool to free up time and mental bandwidth to develop other aspects of their courses. These guidelines should help with prompting AI to create and revise assessments that will serve the course objectives and foster student learning.

  • Provide learning objectives/outcomes. 
  • Assign a role for the AI.
    • "You are a college English instructor teaching freshmen the difference between ethos, pathos, and logos." 
    • "You are a nurse creating a lesson plan about CPR." 
  • Be explicit with instructions. 
    • Type of assessment (formative/summative) 
    • Number and type of questions (multiple choice, true/false, open ended, project-based, etc.) 
    • Required tasks for students to complete 
    • Scaffolding 
  • Use outputs as a starting point. Verify accuracy. 
  • Further prompt the AI to revise. 
    • Add more questions 
    • Add examples 
    • Revise for a different level of student understanding 
    • Add definitions of key terms 
    • Offer feedback to improve the quality, validity, or reliability of the assessment task.

Creating "AI-Proof" Assessments

While using GenAI to enhance student learning is the goal, it is also advisable to design assessments that deter students from using AI to complete assessments and replace learning. These guidelines are best practices for preventing inappropriate uses of AI:

Personalize Assessments

Personalize assessments by asking students to relate the content to their lives or personal experiences. 

Scaffold Assessments

Scaffold assessments by designing smaller assessments that build to a larger assessment. 

Promote Universal Design

Promote Universal Design for Learning by encouraging alternative submission formats, such as video or audio recordings, presentations, projects, portfolios, graphic organizers, etc. 

Utilize Authentic Assessment

Utilize authentic assessments by requiring demonstration of learning in real-world contexts. 

Utilize Metacognition

Utilize Metacognition by asking students to reflect on their own learning process for an assessment. 

Incorporate Class Elements

Incorporate class elements in the assessment. For example, ask students to reflect on a particular discussion from your class, or plan class time for students to work on the assessment. 

Consider a Flipped Model

Consider a flipped model where students are first exposed to new material outside of class, then reinforce their learning through group work, discussions, worksheets, etc. in class. This way, the "homework" is now completed in the class with the instructor and their peers.

Note that this may require a larger time investment than the other strategies listed. 

Assessing with AI

In addition to using AI to create assessments, the instructor might consider creating assessments that require the student to interact with AI. These assessments should not be completed by the AI, but instead utilize AI to assist the student in the completion of the task. Some examples include: 

  • Use an AI image generator to try to create an image of yourself. Practice writing descriptively with specific vivid details to prompt the AI to create an image of your face, and then prompt it to modify the generated image to get closer to your appearance. 
  • Tell a chatbot you are learning a foreign language and want to use it for practice. Ask it to regularly introduce new vocabulary, repeat itself, and correct you in your native language when you make a mistake. 
  • Prompt AI to write an essay that meets the requirements of your essay assignment. Write a response to that essay, analyzing any shortcomings you can find. Is it inventing facts and sources? Is it too repetitive in its sentence structure?  Where does it lack detail or create confusion? Reflect on the potential problems with using AI to replace learning rather than support learning. 
  • Have students analyze an AI generated piece of content to a human-generated piece to spot the differences and justify which is AI and which is human.

2.4 Lesson Planning with an AI Assistant

3 min read

Generative artificial intelligence may be used to support faculty with developing effective lesson plans and activities that can be tailored to their unique context. The guidelines and examples below are meant to provide a framework for developing lesson plans using AI. Faculty are reminded that they are the experts in their areas and these AI tools are merely starting points for the lesson planning. 

Prompting Guidelines

Once you choose the generative AI chatbot for you, consider the guidelines below to begin prompting the AI for lesson planning. The instructor is the content expert. Use outputs as a starting point. Verify accuracy.

1: Provide context

Examples of areas to provide context are

  • Education level (college)
  • Course
    1. Provide course title, not number.
  • Situational factors
    1. Class size
    2. Abilities
    3. Characteristics of Learners
  • Topic/content being taught
  • Student learning outcomes/objectives
  • Role play - Telling the AI to play the role of an expert often improves quality of responses.
    1. "You are an expert in instructional design."
    2. "You are an experienced nursing instructor that utilizes active learning strategies."

2: Define Constraints

Examples of constraints that you can provide are

  • Modality (in-person or online)
  • Time
  • Learning activity preference
    1. Group work
    2. Discussions
    3. Labs
    4. Mini-lectures

3: Expand

The best practice to receive high quality responses is to continue asking questions to expand on the AI's outputs. Some examples are

  • "Give me another example of..."
  • "I'm worried about the warm-up activity because..."
  • "How can I apply the topic to students' lives?"
  • "What misconceptions about the topic may arise?"

3.1 Ensuring Academic Integrity

3 min read

Academic dishonesty is a common and understandable issue where generative AI (GenAI) is involved. Following the below recommendations may alleviate concerns and mitigate academic dishonesty.

Draft an AI Syllabus Statement

Students will likely encounter different instructors with varying stances on allowable use of GenAI. A syllabus statement that clearly defines the expectations for GenAI provides clarity and transparency for students from the beginning. Minimizing ambiguity reduces the cognitive load for students as they navigate a new course, allowing them to focus on the subject matter. 

Additionally, a GenAI syllabus statement ensures fairness and consistency by holding all students to the same standards. It lays the foundation for student accountability and aids the faculty to make decisions if GenAI misuse arises.

Refer to our Developing an AI Syllabus Statement Guide for assistance with drafting an AI syllabus statement.

Talk with Students About AI Early and Often

Using the syllabus statement as a starting point, it is recommended to have conversations with students about appropriate and inappropriate use of GenAI, reasons for the decisions, and potential consequences for abusing GenAI in the course. Some talking points that may facilitate productive conversations are 

  • The expectations and guidelines of AI in the course 
  • The capabilities of AI and its limitations 
    • "Hallucinations" - where AI generates false information or sources (often confidently) 
    • Some models are not trained on up-to-date information 
  • How AI can be used to enhance learning, not replace it 
    • Clarify the learning outcomes of the course and why inappropriate AI use may harm their learning 
  • The bias of AI 
    • Amplifies majority voice based on its training data 
    • Prefers English 
  • The ability to distinguishing GenAI work from human work will become an essential literacy skill.

It is often difficult to find time in a busy class to discuss topics that may not be directly related to the course content. However, many of these talking points can be embedded within assignments and learning activities that can lead to larger conversations. For example: 

  • In a composition course, students may practice peer reviewing with an essay generated by GenAI. 
  • In STEM courses, instructors can prompt GenAI to intentionally provide incorrect information, which students may be asked to fact check. Because GenAI may not have up-to-date training data, it can generate misinformation when new research emerges. 
  • In a health professions course, the instructor may find that a GenAI demonstrates a malpractice while solving a case study. Students can be given the GenAI's solution to identify where the malpractice occurred. 

The University of Michigan has aggregated examples of assignments that use GenAI in a book guide. 

Evaluate Curriculum and Assessment

Given the rapid onset and progress of GenAI, understanding and navigating AI, as well as distinguishing between AI-generated and human-created work will become essential skills for both professional and personal contexts. According to experts, AI will likely replace jobs that are repetitive or remedial, and employers will prioritize candidates that possess AI literacy. The classroom is an ideal setting for students to be able to safely and effectively develop these skills.  

Consider the following questions about course and curriculum designs: 

  • Can GenAI be integrated in the course to enhance student learning? Why or why not? 
  • If GenAI is to be implemented in the course, what are the expectations for appropriate use of AI? 
  • If a student learning outcome can be met easily by GenAI, should the outcome be revised? 
  • Should student learning outcomes include GenAI literacy? 
  • Do assessments in the course authentically measure students' mastery of outcomes?

The Center for Teaching and Learning can support faculty in their course designs.

Assess AI Detection Tools

Mohave Community College has a Turnitin license, which includes their AI detection tool. However, it's important to note that GenAI language models do not copy text verbatim, but rather generate new text based on their training data. So, it is extremely difficult to identify AI writing with certainty. False positives and negatives are possible, and it is relatively easy to bypass AI detection tools. For example, Turnitin's AI detection is based on ChatGPT's language model, but there are several other AI language models that are less likely to be flagged.

AI detection tools should not be used as the sole evidence for punitive measures. Instead, it is recommended to use these tools as a starting point to have a conversation with a student about writing style and academic integrity. An example of how to address students when AI writing is detected:

The assignment dropbox believes this was created using a generative AI like ChatGPT.  If this is true, know that using chatbots to complete assignments for you is plagiarism and all plagiarism is hindering your ability to grow and learn.  In order to get the most out of the class, Artificial intelligence should be used as a tool for enhancing learning and should never be used to replace learning or cheat.  If you did not use a chatbot to write this for you, the detector may be responding to fundamental issues with your writing style that makes it feel like the product of AI.  Your sentence structure is too formulaic and lacks variety.  Your writing lacks necessary details and specific real-world examples.  Your sources may be invented by the AI or not integrated properly into the writing.  These are areas you should continue to work on so the dropbox and other instructors do not accuse you of academic dishonesty.

Intellectual Property an Copyright

To summarize the US Copyright Office's stance and Creative Commons' recommendation: Works without a significant degree of human involvement should be made available to the public domain via CC0. 

Works with significant human involvement may be given other copyright protections, but should cite where and how AI was used.
