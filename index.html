Introduction

Artificial intelligence (AI), specifically generative artificial intelligence (GenAI), is a tool with potential to enhance  effectiveness and efficiency in teaching and learning. However, this field is fast paced and there may be potential for misuse and abuse without proper guidance.

These resources will serve as a preliminary guide that establishes working parameters for Artificial Intelligence within instruction at Mohave Community College. This includes defining AI, effectively and safely applying AI to teaching and learning, and addressing ethical considerations of AI in higher education.

It is not the scope of this guide to explore the technical aspects of AI. The guide will focus on what the taskforce views as best practices for engaging with GenAI in higher education. The guide will also seek to follow federal laws, best practices, and internal policies and procedures. Due to the constant evolution of these tools, this guide may be updated and revised as the technology and pedagogy advance.

Faculty are encouraged to approach AI with a growth mindset in teaching and learning. This means believing that one's abilities can improve through learning over time. One should engage with AI as an opportunity to learn rather than something to fear or avoid.

The growth mindset was popularized by Stanford psychology professor, Carol Dweck.

This guide was developed by a taskforce representing all instructional divisions, the Center for Teaching and Learning, Information Technology, and Employee Services.

1.1 Definitions

2 min read

Cornell University defines generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) as "a subset of AI that utilizes machine learning models to create new, original content, such as images, text, or music, based on patterns and structures learned from existing data."

Drawbacks and Considerations

Similar to other technologies, there are pros and cons of GenAI. Below are some drawbacks and considerations when using GenAI:

Bias

GenAI models mimic the data on which they are trained. Training data often contains mostly voices from majority populations. Without intentional diversification of training data, GenAI models will amplify majority voices, opinions, and sentiments.

Disinformation

Most text-based GenAI models, like ChatGPT, do not search for relevant sources in databases online. Instead, they use advanced statistics to predict the most likely next word – one word at a time. This can lead to inaccurate responses, often called hallucinations.

It also allows malicious actors to create realistic images, documents, and sources that may be used to intentionally deceive people.

Privacy

Review data use policies of the GenAI tool you plan to use. Only input data that are appropriate to share publicly and externally to MCC. Exercise caution when working with private, sensitive, or identifiable information, and avoid sharing any student information (which could be a FERPA violation), third party copyrighted materials, or any materials that you do not own or manage the rights to.

(Paraphrased from the UNLV Library)

Caution

Due to these issues, it is the user's responsibility to verify the accuracy of GenAI responses.

Text Generative AI Tools

Text generative AI tools, or 'text-to-text', are tools that receive text inputs from the user and generate text outputs to the user. They can often sound human-like in their responses and will present themselves as helpful assistants. Some popular text-to-text tools are:
  •  ChatGPT is arguably the most well-known GenAI tool out there. It is a chatbot developed by OpenAI. There is a free version (ChatGPT 3.5) and a paid version (ChatGPT 4), which has more features and is tied to other GenAI tools, like DALL-E 3 (see image generative tools).
  •  Claude is GenAI chatbot developed by Anthropic. Anthropic prides themselves on their Constitutional AI model that is employed in Claude. According to Anthropic, Constitutional AI "AI enables AI systems to generate useful responses while also minimizing harm." Claude also has a free version and a paid version with more features.
  •  Google Gemini (formerly Google Bard) is Google's GenAI chatbot. You may interact with the chatbot on the web. However, Google has been integrating Gemini into more of it's products, like Google Assistant and Maps, to improve the user experience. Gemini is free.
  •  Microsoft Copilot is Microsoft's GenAI chatbot. Copilot is powered by OpenAI's ChatGPT-4 and DALL-E 3, which you normally have to pay for, but is free when running through Copilot. Microsoft has integrated Copilot into Windows desktops, the Edge browser, Bing, and their own mobile app. However, there are paid features of Copilot such as integration with Microsoft 365 apps and use of a faster model.

Image Generative Tools

Image generative tools, or 'text-to-image', receive text inputs from the user and generate images to the user that match the description. The text inputs can be natural language descriptions of images such as "An astronaut riding cowboy riding a horse" (seriously, try that out). Some popular text-to-image tools are:
  •  Stable Diffusion is a text-to-image model developed by Stability AI. Unlike most AI models, they released their source code to the public, providing more transparency and customization to users. Stable Diffusion also has an image editing tool. Stable Diffusion is free for users, and they offer some paid services to developers leveraging their model. Dream Studio is a sleek interface that leverages the Stable Diffusion model.
  •  Midjourney is a paid text-to-image model developed by an independent research lab solely focused on this one product. Midjourney is generally seen as one of the leaders in the field of text-to-image AIs.
  •  ImageFX is Google's text-to-image model. The tools is free and easy-to-use tool that is more accessible than most text-to-image models. However, it does not provide as much customization and freedom as other tools.
  •  DALL-E is a text-to-image model developed by OpenAI (the same developers of ChatGPT). It runs on the same model as ChatGPT. OpenAI is focusing their efforts to their newer model, DALL-E 3, which is part of the paid subscription package of ChatGPT. However, users can generate images on the DALL-E 3 model by using Microsoft Copilot for free.

Media Editing Tools

Video and voice editing and generating models are not as available as text and image generative models due to their complexity. There are some text-to-video models in development, but not readily available to the public. Below is a list of some media (video/voice) AI tools:
  •  Adobe Premiere Pro is a widely used video editing platform that has been developing their own AI features for video editing. It includes features such as speech-to-text, speech enhancement, scene detection, and more. Adobe Premiere Pro is a paid product; however, MCC has limited licenses available for employees. Submit an IT ticket to request an account.
  •  Murf AI is a text-to-speech tool specifically designed for media creation and voice overs on videos. There is a free version with very basic features, and paid licenses with more features and integrations.
  •  Synthesia is an AI video generator that uses AI avatars in place of actors or voice overs. The avatars themselves are each based on a real actor who has licensed their likeness to Synthesia. The tool is only available with a paid subscription.

2.1 Developing an AI Syllabus Statement

2 min read

At Mohave Community College, instructors are allowed the freedom to decide how to incorporate generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) into their classrooms, if at all. It is recommended that instructors communicate their decisions regarding GenAI expectations in the course syllabus. Below are examples and guiding questions to help develop an AI syllabus statement. These guidelines were developed by a taskforce representing all instructional divisions, the Center for Teaching and Learning, Information Technology, and Employee Services.

No Generative AI Allowed

[The instructor may modify the example statement below according to their curriculum.] 

Example Statement

Generative artificial intelligence is a tool that can create text, images, and other content. It is natural to feel stressed or overwhelmed from schoolwork, but do not use AI chatbots to complete tasks for this class. AI has serious limitations, and relying on it to complete work for you will hinder your ability to master the course material and achieve the course outcomes. Please contact your instructor for assistance with your assignments and for clarification on which tools are appropriate to use in this course.

Generative AI Allowed

Guidelines and Questions to Consider

  • Before drafting an AI syllabus statement, familiarize yourself with AI resources and its limitations.
  • How might students use AI to enhance learning in the context of their class?
  • What AI tools are allowed for this class?
  • May students use the AI tools on all assignments, or only specific assignments?
  • What are inappropriate student uses of AI?
  • What are the consequences for inappropriate AI usage?
  • What attributions of AI are expected?

Example Statement

[The instructor may modify the example statement below according to their curriculum.] 

Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) is a new and fast-changing technology we are all learning to ethically and effectively use. Cornell University defines Generative artificial intelligence as "a subset of AI that utilizes machine learning models to create new, original content, such as images, text, or music, based on patterns and structures learned from existing data." Abuse of AI will be treated as academic dishonesty, but by respecting the following guidelines, we can grow and learn together in this uncharted area.

Artificial intelligence chatbots can be a great tool to enhance learning. You may use GenAI in this course to

  • Assist with brainstorming, such as outline generation
  • Proofread
  • Generate study aides
  • Fill gaps in your understanding of a topic.

However, one of the purposes of education is to improve critical thinking and problem solving, and AI should not replace that process. The following are inappropriate uses of AI.

  • Asking AI to complete an entire assignment
  • Submitting AI-generated content in written work without proper citation
  • Asking AI to answer assessment questions

When using AI in this course, follow these general guidelines.

  • Provide your prompting process and cite your use of AI by following the [APA MLA, Chicago] format
  • Verify the accuracy of AI outputs because it is common for AI to produce incorrect information and sources
  • Contact your instructor if you are unsure if your use of AI is appropriate
